What DynaLab is for

DynaLab helps you ask mechanical questions about a protein: how it deforms under tension, which parts become exposed at different loads, and how those insights can feed structure-based binder ideas and bench experiments. The core idea is mechanobiology—many proteins experience forces in tissue; their hidden (cryptic) binding surfaces can appear only when the chain is stretched or partially unfolded.

You do not need to write code to use the app. This guide explains the science behind each step: what the simulation is approximating, what the plots mean, and how to interpret results when planning experiments or discussing findings with collaborators.

Coarse-grained simulations

DynaLab drives Upside2, a coarse-grained (CG) protein model. Instead of every atom, the representation keeps enough degrees of freedom to capture backbone mechanics and secondary-structure tendencies at a fraction of the cost of all-atom MD. That makes it realistic to explore many forces, replicas, and long timelines—exactly the regime where mechanical unfolding and exposure transitions show up.

The trade-off is resolution: side-chain packing, explicit solvent, and many chemical details are simplified or absent. Exposure-style metrics are proxies, not crystallography. Use CG to form hypotheses (which regions move, in what order, near which loads), then validate with higher-resolution methods or experiment where it matters.

Thermal dynamics (no pull)

Constant temperature simulations let the chain fluctuate around its native basin. You use them to check stability, sampling, and baseline flexibility before adding load. Replica exchange (when enabled) swaps replicas at different temperatures to cross energy barriers a single temperature might not cross—useful for broad conformational exploration, not combined with pulling in this workflow.

Independent replicas at the same settings are separate “parallel experiments”: they help you see run-to-run variability, which is especially important when pathways are stochastic.

Mechanical pulling

Constant tension

A fixed load is applied along a direction you configure (often end-to-end). The protein sits under sustained force and fluctuates; over time you may see partial unfolding, domain peeling, or exposure of buried loops. This is the closest computational analogue to assays where force is held near constant—conceptually aligned with how a multi-lane centrifuge can expose the same construct to different steady loads.

Velocity clamp (AFM-style)

One end moves at a controlled speed while the other is fixed. The recorded force–extension behavior resembles single-molecule AFM traces: unfolding appears as sawtooth-like features when domains resist then suddenly extend. Use this mode when your scientific story is about pathway and rupture forces under controlled pulling speed, not strictly constant-load steady states.

Force sweeps & statistics

A force sweep runs the same pulling setup at several target forces (a “ladder”), optionally with multiple replicas per force. Scientifically, you are mapping load → response: at which forces does extension jump, do secondary-structure signals change, or do exposure proxies spike?

Replicas matter because unfolding is stochastic: two identical runs can differ in timing and pathway. Treat replicas as repeated experiments; compare distributions, not single traces, when drawing conclusions.

Recommended workflow

Think in terms of questions, not file names:

  1. Structure in — Upload your target PDB and set simulation length and temperature sensibly for the size of the system and the phenomena you care about.
  2. Mechanics — Enable pulling or a force sweep; choose tension vs velocity clamp to match the experimental analogy you have in mind.
  3. Observe — After a run completes, use Run Analysis to quantify compactness, flexibility, contacts, exposure, etc., along time.
  4. Sweep-wide picture (force sweeps only) — Use Compute Epitope Candidates to summarize how exposure and epitope-style candidates evolve across the whole ladder, not just one force.
  5. Representative shapes — Clustering (per trajectory or via the sweep rollup) picks a small set of structures that stand in for major visited states.
  6. All-atom bridge — Back-mapping rebuilds full-atom coordinates so downstream tools that expect atomic detail can run.
  7. Binder hypotheses — The AI design step proposes nanobody-like scaffolds and sequences against a chosen snapshot and hotspot hints; outputs are ranked models, not guaranteed drugs.
  8. Bench alignment — Generate an experiment sheet and, once you have wet-lab readouts, compare fluorescence (or similar) vs force to where simulations predicted transitions.
Epitope rollup vs clustering: You can obtain representative structures from per-trajectory clustering alone. The sweep rollup adds a joint view across forces and is especially useful for sweep-wide epitope and burial plots— it complements, rather than replaces, reading individual force/replica analyses.

What each analysis measures

Each checkbox in Post-processing analysis answers a different scientific question about the trajectory you select.

AnalysisScientific questionHow to read it
Rg (radius of gyration)Is the chain expanding or compacting over time?Upward trends often accompany unfolding or swelling; combine with other metrics.
RMSDHow far has the structure drifted from the starting frame?Large RMSD can mean real rearrangement or rigid-body motion; align interpretation with visuals.
RMSFWhich residues fluctuate most?Peaks highlight hinges and loops; under force, flexible epitope-bearing loops may spike before exposure events.
End-to-endHow stretched is the chain between termini (or defined ends)?Central readout for pulling: relates directly to extension under load.
H-bonds / salt bridgesAre stabilizing contacts breaking?Loss of native contacts often tracks unfolding stages; CG proxies are qualitative.
ShapeIs the molecule’s mass distribution spherical, rod-like, or asymmetric?Useful alongside Rg for classifying compact vs extended states.
Cross-correlationWhich parts move together?Blocks suggest domains or coupled motions; helps interpret collective behavior.
Secondary structureWhere are helices and sheets, and do they persist?Loss of sheet or helix content under load supports mechanical unfolding narratives.
PCAWhat are the dominant collective motions?First components often capture opening/closing; useful for summarizing large trajectories.
Force vs extensionWhat load–extension relationship occurred?Pulling-specific; compare qualitative shape to AFM-like expectations.
Contact mapWhich residue pairs touch often?Shows native contact loss and new contacts; computationally heavier.
Burial scanWhen do side chains become more solvent-exposed?Central to cryptic epitope thinking: buried grooves can open under tension.
Dihedral unfoldingAre backbone Ramachandran basins shifting?Captures gradual loss of native φ/ψ preferences vs a single SS label.
ClusteringHow many distinct shapes did we visit?Writes representative PDBs for later back-mapping and design; medoids are scientific choices, not ground truth.

Exposure, epitopes & sweep rollups

A cryptic epitope is a site that is sterically or energetically hidden when the protein is relaxed but can become reachable when mechanics open the structure. In simulation, “cryptic epitope candidates” are usually identified from patterns in exposure proxies as a function of time and/or force—not from immunology directly.

For force sweeps, Run Analysis gives you one block of plots per force and replica: ideal for “what happened at 22 pN on replica 2?”. Compute Epitope Candidates adds a sweep-wide layer: which residues rank highly when you consider the ladder together, and how burial-like signals vary across force. Use both: per-run detail plus global summary.

Structures & “intermediates”

A trajectory contains thousands of frames; clustering picks a small set of representative structures (often called intermediates in folding and unfolding literature) that approximate major states: native-like, partially unfolded, highly extended, etc. Those snapshots are what you later back-map and optionally present to a binder model as the “target face” under mechanical stress.

Per-trajectory clustering summarizes one run. Joint sweep clustering (part of the epitope rollup) pools information across completed sweep trajectories to find representatives that span the whole mechanical experiment. Both write coarse structures the app lists before back-mapping; they answer slightly different scientific questions.

Back-mapping

Coarse models are excellent for mechanics at scale; most antibody and nanobody design pipelines expect all-atom coordinates with realistic bond chemistry for at least the target. Back-mapping reconstructs heavy atoms from the coarse backbone using geometric rules, then optionally relaxes clashes. The result is a usable hypothesis structure, not a crystal structure—always inspect in a molecular viewer before over-interpreting side chains.

AI binder design

The design stage combines generative backbone sampling, sequence design, and structure prediction / scoring (via the Tamarind integration). You choose a back-mapped snapshot and hotspot residues—typically residues your exposure and sweep analyses highlighted—to bias the binder toward biologically plausible contacts.

The UI can request many backbone variants and several sequences per backbone. That produces a large ranked pool of in silico candidates (scores such as predicted interface confidence). Treat the output as hypotheses for synthesis or further modeling, ranked suggestions—not a count of validated therapeutics. Start small while learning the workflow; expand the search when you understand cost and noise.

Experiments & comparison

The centrifuge-oriented experiment sheet turns your simulation-informed forces and controls into a bench-oriented protocol narrative: zones, loads, controls, and attachment chemistry described in language a lab can execute.

After you collect fluorescence (or similar) vs force with replicates and conditions, you can upload a tidy CSV and overlay it with the simulation-derived threshold you care about. The comparison plot supports the scientific loop: did the system respond near the load the model predicted? Disagreement is informative—it may mean calibration, timescale, construct differences, or limitations of the CG model.

Force units & calibration

Single-protein mechanics lives in the piconewton (pN) range—tiny by everyday standards but huge relative to thermal noise at the nanoscale. The app can display and schedule forces in pN after a calibration that maps the simulation’s internal tension units to physical force using a reference system (similar in spirit to calibrating an instrument against a known standard). Revisit calibration when the force field, integrator settings, or target class changes substantially.

Membrane & restraints

Membrane options approximate confinement or a slab geometry so membrane-associated targets can be oriented with a hydrophobic midplane in mind. This is still CG: use it when the scientific question benefits from z (or curvature) constraints, and coordinate the PDB orientation with the geometry you choose.

Restraints (distance locks, walls, springs, nails) let you mimic experimental anchors, keep domains from diffusing apart unrealistically, or test “what if this contact were held?” scenarios. They change the effective free energy landscape— document them clearly when reporting results.

Using the app (practical)

  • Settings — Stores your external AI provider credentials locally on the server machine for real design runs; mock mode is available for training without cloud charges.
  • Upload & basic simulation — Define temperature, length, and whether you want replica exchange or multiple independent replicas.
  • Pulling & force sweep — Configure load geometry; enable sweep for a force ladder. Watch sub-job progress until the sweep completes.
  • Membrane & restraints — Optional; enable when your biological setup needs them.
  • Run — Starts the simulation job; when finished, results and analysis sections unlock.
  • Post-processing analysis — Select metrics that match your hypothesis, then run. For sweeps, expand rows per force/replica.
  • Force sweep rollups — Appears only after a sweep; use Compute Epitope Candidates for ladder-wide summaries. The ? next to the heading opens detailed help.
  • Back-mapping — Run once you have representative coarse PDBs listed.
  • AI nanobody design — Choose snapshot and hotspots; review ranked candidates before any wet work.
  • Centrifuge experiment & comparison — Generate the sheet, run the lab, upload data, compare to prediction.

Inline ? help buttons throughout the app mirror short and long explanations similar in spirit to this guide.

Limitations & caution

  • Resolution — CG exposes large-scale mechanics; fine epitope chemistry and solvent structure need other methods.
  • Single trajectories — Long runs may still miss rare events; sweeps and replicas reduce but do not eliminate that risk.
  • AI scores — Predicted complex scores are models with known failure modes; treat top hits as leads for validation.
  • Back-mapping — Reconstruction introduces stereochemical uncertainty; minimize and visually inspect before publication figures.

Glossary

  • Coarse-grained (CG) MD — Molecular dynamics with simplified particles and forces; faster, lower resolution than all-atom.
  • Cryptic epitope — Binding surface hidden at rest that can appear under mechanical or allosteric opening (used loosely here for exposure-driven leads).
  • Replica — Independent repeat of the same simulation conditions; quantifies stochasticity.
  • Force sweep — Family of runs at different loads to map mechanical response.
  • Medoid / representative — Cluster center used as a snapshot stand-in for a group of similar frames.
  • ipTM / pTM / pLDDT — Model-reported confidence and interface metrics for ranked AI-designed complexes.
  • pN — Piconewton, ~10⁻¹² N; typical scale for single-molecule pulling after calibration.
